I recently finished reading the book, Linked[Audible] by Albert-Laszlo Barabasi[Wikipedia]. I found the book incredibly interesting, and would recommend it to anyone that is interested in network theory or the internet on a low level. In short, the book details the evolution of network theory; its past, current applications and its future.

The first part of the book is a little dry, but definitely not to the point that I didn't want to continue. The evolution of network theory is followed from its very beginnings, as graphs of paths, to current applications in complex system modelling.

Initially, network theory was quite simple. Networks were considered to be randomly formed. This worked in principal but when applied to existing networks it could not account for certain results. Barabasi's team was involved in new advances in network theory, namely the scale-free model and preferential attachment. These advances, and a few others, form a large part of the current network theory model and can mostly describe many of today's complex systems; cell metabolism, the brain, the internet, social networks.

Other topics, such as "six degrees of separation" and virtual and social "hubs" are discussed as well. There are some interesting stories included that you would not immediately expect to be related to network theory. These often help to illustrate the theoretical point just covered too.

Once again, I recommend this book to anyone interested in networks in general, the internets workings, physicists, microbiologists, and anyone keen to expand their knowledge.
